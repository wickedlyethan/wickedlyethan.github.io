I can’t think of a more simple way to point to the broken social contract than [blasting your phone in public](https://arstechnica.com/culture/2026/03/explain-it-like-im-5-why-is-everyone-on-speakerphone-in-public/). It's an unrefutably obnoxious and indefensible public behavior that is somehow ubiquitous, yet there's no world in which I'd confront someone over it. My catastrophizing brain jumps right to "they're gonna start an argument and you're going to get filmed, clipped, and doxxed in no time."

Put these thoughts next to recent Pew surveys that shows that most Americans think other Americans are [unethical assholes](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2026/03/05/in-25-country-survey-americans-especially-likely-to-view-fellow-citizens-as-morally-bad/) and [most people can't be trusted](https://www.pewresearch.org/2025/05/08/americans-trust-in-one-another/) and you paint an ugly and asymmetrical picture. 

As silly as it sounds, something as annoying but technically harmless as inflicting your audio on others in public feels like a low bar that can't be cleared. It reinforces the negative thought cycles precisely *because* it's so easy to not be like this.
