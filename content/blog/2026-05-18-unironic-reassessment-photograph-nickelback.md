*The Long Road* was once of the first CDs I ever had. I loved the heavy wall of sound and the huge, fast drum parts compared to the dad-rock I had been exposed to up until then – Santana had nothing like [“Flat On The Floor”](https://music.youtube.com/watch?v=sf6cm14mNP4&si=llav3_SR4S5hS2it), obviously. I listened to Nickelback unironically for years, because irony wasn’t a thing for ten year olds in the 2000s, thank fucking goodness. But suddenly the Canadian grunge rock band became rapidly uncool and I hit the age where you care about that. I punted the three CDs I had at the end of my collection, part of a growing shame corner; I deleted them from my mp3 player; I stopped hammering their songs into my drum kit.

There are plenty of valid reasons to dislike Nickelback, like any band – their lyrics are frequently grossly misogynistic and their choice of genre is “of its time”. Their emotional scope is narrow, the songs can sound similar, etcetera. But pop culture, as informed by the spinning toilet vortex of the memetic Internet, doesn’t engage with anything like a good faith critique, and the reason is their 2005 mega-hit [“Photograph”](https://music.youtube.com/watch?v=LzejHuBS_D4&si=bDz5v96yM-YTYgUN).

It was on in a grocery store of all things the other day and I was overcome with a wave of emotion, the exact payload of nostalgia per its Greek roots of “homecoming” (*nóstos*) and “pain” (*álgos*). The song is the butt of so many jokes – from [shit photoshops](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/nickelbacks-photograph) to overlong [*Deadpool* drivel](https://youtu.be/Qgh4wSxAKqo?si=BcfXQG_z9z5z13gt) to getting DMCA’d after the President of the United States used it to [spread conspiracy theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photograph_(Nickelback_song)#Use_by_Donald_Trump) – that I’d avoided it for years even though I always liked it. I didn’t expect to be attacked by it while picking out seltzer, but oh well.

Listening to it again, “Photograph” is a vulnerable, apologetic, soaring, and deeply sad song. (It’s damn well produced, too.) It’s simple autobiography, and the details give it authenticity that make it undeniable – Kroeger sings about breaking into his school at night, his local arcade burning to the ground, dreams of being a rockstar – but they’re the easiest targets, blood in the water if you’re looking to hide from the core of the track. The last lines of each verse lend devastating universal weight to the anecdotes:

> [First verse]\
I wonder if it's too late\
Should I go back and try to graduate?\
Life's better now than it was back then\
If I was them, I wouldn't let me in

> [Second verse]\
Kim's the first girl I kissed\
I was so nervous that I nearly missed\
She's had a couple of kids since then\
I haven't seen her since God knows when

> [Bridge]\
If I could relive those days\
I know the one thing that would never change

Kroeger has already accepted the truth – “Photograph” isn’t about the homecoming, it’s about the *pain*. The lead-in to each chorus is “Oh God, I”, sung in seeking relief from the heartache, looking for the strength to do what the chorus says: “It's hard to say it, time to say it / Goodbye, goodbye”. It’s not accidental that the final lines re-iterate the beginning but don’t complete it: 

> Look at this photograph\
Every time I do, it makes me laugh\
Every time I do, it makes me

What else does it make him do? What does an old photograph do to *you*? Cry? Get pissed? Throw it in a fire? Put it in a frame? Smile – big, small, bent at the edges, trying to cover up something else?

The antidote to an allergy to earnestness can be as simple as getting offline and as complicated as wrestling with your fears, and “Photograph” is a primer course. Are you afraid you fucked up a whole host of years? Do you miss people that don’t exist anymore? Do you miss a version of *yourself* that doesn’t exist anymore? Or do you have trouble reconciling that you once were shitty, that you can’t do life “right”?

Per the bridge, “You can't erase / You can't replace it”, and you shouldn’t try. You don’t even need to keep the photograph – you can say goodbye – but you do need to accept what happens every time you look at it.
