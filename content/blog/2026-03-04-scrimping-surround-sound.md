When I was a kid my dad got a Panasonic home theater in a box kit to go with our 32” CRT and our DVD/VHS combo player. For the time it was luxurious, as we were solidly middle class; a handful of the rich kids I went to school with had surround sound to go with their rear projection or freaking plasma TVs. My dad loved movies and he loved that we loved movies.

Unfortunately for him I loved movies so much that I woke up at the crack of dawn the next day, after spending all evening setting the system up but not testing it out, and learned the loud way that some amplifiers start the volume level in the negatives. I cranked *Jurassic Park* to the literal maximum (0.0 db) and let that T-Rex roar to welcome the day. No one else in the house was pleased, but I sure was.

That system fell apart pretty quickly because it was a hunk of junk from Circuit City; the surrounds gathered dust and the subwoofer was flicked off the majority of the time. Twenty years later and I have cash but I hate spending it, so I tried to scrounge up what I needed to give our humble 3.0 speaker setup the surround sound sauce on a budget. It didn’t go well on the first try, but now we’ve got a handsome, unobtrusive setup I’m thrilled with.

My first stop is always craigslist. One might call me savant of the site and it also comes from my dad, who was a fiend for the printed “want ad” classifieds that he’d pick up at the Tedeschi’s gas station. 

There are always too many speakers on craigslist, but I was on the hunt for small satellite speakers and stands for them. I struck out on both, technically, but I wouldn’t figure that out until I met up with a dude in the Market Basket parking lot to find out that the Sony SS-SR100 speakers were not only unremarkable bookshelf speakers from a vintage home theater in a box kit ironically, they were way too big. They overwhelmed the Best Buy-brand [satellite speaker stands](https://www.bestbuy.com/product/insignia-28-38-adjustable-height-speaker-stands-for-satellite-speakers-black/J2FPJKSCFG) I had settled on after finding nothing on craigslist.

They were so big that the only viable mounting solution were weak-ass double-sided sticky pads that came with the stands, the equivalent of throwing your hands in the air and going “well I hope this works”. Well it didn’t, because I put the pads too far back and the speakers became front heavy and fell off in the middle of the night, the squeaky spring clip terminals too weak to hold on to the wire and let us get a full night’s sleep. The Sonys had felt temporary from the moment I tested them out thanks to both Emily’s withering reaction to their intrusion into our cozy living room and their aggressive, shouty sound, but I was hoping to get *some* time out of them. I had dreamed of doing surround sound under $100, but without spending an inordinate amount of time in local thrift shops it wasn’t looking likely. 

I chose the adorable [Cambridge Audio Minx Min 12s](https://www.crutchfield.com/S-YBVLCFfwGJU/p_779MIN12B/Cambridge-Audio-Minx-Min-12-Black.html?tp=186), tiny cube speakers that are designed to be satellites in the first place, though they could be really cute desktop speakers when paired with a Class D desktop amp. For $99 each the Minx Min 12s are lively, present but not obnoxious, and lovely to look at in part because there isn’t much to look at. I’m going to unscientifically guess they are 1/6 the size of the Sonys and maybe 10% of the size of our main speakers, the wonderful and eagerly precise Wharfedale Diamond 12s.

They include removable binding post terminals that let me use banana plugs, which are a huge quality of life upgrade. My Sony STR-DH590 receiver annoyingly gives binding post terminals for the stereo speakers only, spring clip terminals for the rest, but anything to reduce the time spent twirling bare wire. They mounted easily (though upside down) and dare I say I even had fun installing them and the banana plugs. More fun, certainly, than threading the speaker wire under the rug by myself had been.

To test the system I wanted something fun and pseudo-scientific; I found the incredible [Demolandia](https://www.demolandia.net/) archive of historical home theater demos and loved perusing it. Classic THX, Dolby Digital, and IMAX demos and theater pre-shows available for download and to scare the crap out of your cats with. These are a lot of fun.

I followed that up with scenes from *Dune* (2021) and *Predator: Badlands* (2025) and honestly was blown away. Yes elements whizz by, but more importantly surrounds fill the space and are instantly immersive. The texture is nice, but the presence is key. The dust of Arrakis surrounds you, the demonic vines of Genna snap and twist near your ears, and the respective scores hammer you into the couch and sweep you into the scenes. It’s magnificent and I can’t wait to revisit favorites. I haven’t even unwrapped my 4k blu-ray of *Master and Commander: The Far Side of the World* (2003) knowing that I wanted to do this first. I haven’t tested games or TV shows like *The Pitt* yet either (I had never even conceived of TV shows in 5.1, it’s exciting!).

All told I spent a touch under $300 on adding surrounds to our setup, which isn’t as thrifty as I wanted it to be, but in the world of hi-fi is a pittance and the ROI is a massive grin on my face.
