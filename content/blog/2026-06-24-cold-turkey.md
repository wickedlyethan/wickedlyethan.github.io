title: Bubbles and bears and POSSE, oh my
date: 2026-06-24T07:45:19-04:00
---

I'm on day not-actually-sure of quitting Reddit cold turkey and it feels great, in that nothing of value has been lost. On mobile I use [One Sec](https://one-sec.app/) to introduce a whopping 90 seconds of waiting to open Reddit, which is enough to kill the urge. On desktop I continue to use [Leechblock](https://www.proginosko.com/leechblock/) to block Reddit all the time; I not only have to choose to unblock it, I have to give myself a set amount of time. This is great because I can hit up a niche subreddit for research when I need to, but it’s enough friction that I can't idly scroll to avoid having a single original thought.

Reddit continues to have its hold on me because it, despite all evidence to the contrary, claims to be where people are. It's not; it's a bot-infested hellhole full of low-quality reposts and Daily Beast vomit.

But I think I've found a few new places where people actually are and I'm enjoying them a lot lately:

I'm surprised that I enjoy browsing [Hacker News](https://news.ycombinator.com/) as much as I do, but the comments are smarmy and specific, so that at least feels real and for every ten posts about a novel way to create a PostgreSQL whatever there's cool software being shared and adversarial big-tech hot takes.

Pinboard's [Popular feed](http://pinboard.in/popular) ends up having a lot of similar links to Hacker News – there's a lot of overlap to that Venn diagram – but the extra effort indicated by users bookmarking the links sort of validates them. I’ve found a lot of cool tools this way.

The latest find, and the one that's making me rethink the internet in a positive way for the first time in a long time, is [Bubbles.town](https://bubbles.town/). It's an aggregator of IndieWeb posts with community voting, but somehow it's not the same drivel about switching CMS's or "why I blog" navel-gazing – there's writing with variety. I love this post about a [humble](https://imperfect.bearblog.dev/prettiest-girl-today/) single page site, a trend of sharing [9 Steam games you think need more love](https://blog.curiousquail.com/the-9-games-thing/), and a tiny slice of [life in a seaside town](https://forkingmad.blog/my-neighbour-tells-me-i-am-crazy/). I'm struck by how *short* posts can be! What I call "notes" are posts for others like any other. Maybe I should be slapping a title on them and putting it all in the same feed...

One of the weirdest outcomes of stumbling upon these new feeds of people’ posts is it makes me want to build my website *less*, not more. The technical interconnections of the IndieWeb confuse me, and platforms like [bearblog](https://bearblog.dev/), [Pagecord](https://pagecord.com/), and [Micro.blog](https://micro.blog/) take care of those for you and seem to be popular. Does that defeat the spirit? Does it lean into it? How important is it for you to fully "own" your content, and is that what I'm doing if I'm using a GitHub repo, renting space on Microsoft servers that's being constantly crawled by AI? My own code minimalism feels like it's preventing me from being part of these spaces, not helping, nevermind the limits on my knowledge. 

I could [syndicate](https://indieweb.org/POSSE) in theory, or make sure I join a platform that has full Markdown export so I can take my shit when I leave (bearblog doesn't, sadly).

Or, because this is always part of my thought process, I could delete every trace of myself online and never post again. Maybe like Reddit I would feel like nothing has been lost. Lots to think about!

P.S. I’ve hit semantic satiation with the phrase “cold turkey” while writing this post. What a weird idiom.
