I got to see [Tigers Jaw](https://tigersjaw.bandcamp.com) with nathan at The Sinclair on Monday and they put on an incredible show. I’m new to them and didn’t know any of the older tracks that made the crowd go feral[^1], but what better introduction to them then a sold out show singing along.

Tigers Jaw goes a lot harder live than the records can convey. Lead and co-founder Ben Walsh carefully restrains his voice on records, and the production on the two latest albums is warm and rolled off, which can make tracks sound too similar. None of that is present live – Walsh used a semi-hollow guitar and distorted it to hell, which gave it incredible bite that reminded me more of Brian Baker from Bad Religion than American Football[^2]; the bass was a deadly accurate gut punch; and Brianna Collins dialed up the reverb on her synths which gave everything a great wash alongside her vocals.

The exception to this is Teddy fuckin’ Roberts, Tigers Jaw’s drummer and literal Viking. He’s got to be six feet tall, with long hair and a sleeveless shirt behind a kit that is pure thunder and lightning. I was pumped to see him play live and his sound translated perfectly, which is nuts because his sound is *nuts*.

Check out the intro to “Commit” from *‌I Won't Care How You Remember Me* (2021):

It’s a simple but propulsive rudiment traveling around the kit but the sound is *massive*. It’s even better at the end of the track with that machine gun snare finish. This is the track that made me take notice.

On the new album, “Head is Like a Sinking Stone” was the single that sold me on getting tickets and has an addictive bridge with an offset hi-hat splash and Roberts’ floor tom thundering in at the *beginning* of the second half of the phrase, accenting the difference between “time” and  “truth” in the lyrics. It’s fucking brilliant.

All over the album his fills meander around the toms and refuse to stick to simple 16ths and it makes them feel like storms rolling over the plains, or thunder right over your head. Live he dials the intensity it up to a hurricane, almost knocking over his main tom and battering the floor tom. It shoves the whole band to push themselves, and at times hovers right on the edge of chaos. Even when the band goes down tempo, like on the sublime [“Baptized on a Redwood Drive”](https://tigersjaw.bandcamp.com/track/baptized-on-a-redwood-drive), the snare cracks and the bass drum gallops next to it with no change in intensity and punctuating fills like earthquakes.

Roberts uses a custom kit from [Blackwood Drums](https://www.blackwooddrums.com/artists?lightbox=dataItem-k3qe65sc) that I’m pretty sure is a freaking [aluminum hybrid](https://www.blackwooddrums.com/product-page/aluminum-kit) with a [brass snare](https://www.instagram.com/p/CeQB0WLsx6C/). I’m vindicated that I correctly guessed it was a metal kit – there’s so much attack and so little singing that it had to be, nevermind needing a kit to keep up with this guy – but I’ve never heard a metal kit sound this good! Again, it’s lightning and thunder. That [snare](https://www.instagram.com/theodoreroberts/reel/DWSN4C0jtG7/) might even be 7” deep, cranked all the way up, and the floor tom (my love) is as big as a jazz kit’s bass drum with [some muffling](https://www.instagram.com/theodoreroberts/reel/DWcZtnaDmfi/) on it to keep it focused on blowing your ribs out.

Tigers Jaw was great, live music is awesome, seeing artists you only kind of know is sick, and drummers are under appreciated. The worst part about seeing a show this good is that I just sold my thrashed electronic kit and I can’t play until I get a new one. I miss acoustic drums like a phantom limb, which feels appropriately emo.

[^1]: I haven't been to a show with that kind of energy in a long time. My most recent concert was [bbno$](/blog/2026/03/bbno-internet-explorer-tour/) and on the day I went to Tigers Jaw I bought tickets for Emily and I see to see the music from *Outlander* played live by an orchestra, lol

[^2]: I went with American Football for this reference because I learned that Tigers Jaw is part of a vanguard of sorts for “fourth wave emo”. I’m not a music historian, I find endless genre labels maddening, and I just want to write about loud drums. Don’t yell at me.
