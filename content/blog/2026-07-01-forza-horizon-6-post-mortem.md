The biggest setback for *Forza Horizon 6* was me perusing my [favorite screenshots](/blog/2026/05/best-horizon-5-photos/) from *Horizon 5*. In that post I wrote: 

> So far *Horizon* 6 is tickling my fancy but feels toned down and sometimes even stiff. The neon isn’t quite neon enough, the mud not thick and slinging, the sun shy and reserved. Maybe that’s Playground Games wanting to avoid the most egregious version of a *Nippon*-Epcot vibe, but we’ll see if that changes as we get further into it and the seasons change.

It didn’t, unfortunately. The vibes stayed the same: reserved, low-contrast, played straight, kept polite. I hesitate to call it boring or lifeless, but that’s where I parked at the end of forty-two hours of blandly driving the countryside.

There are brilliant additions to *Horizon* that are worth mentioning:

- The dual-track campaign is great and let me feel like any way I chose to play that day was rewarding.
- The regional mascots were fun to track down and make funny faces when you smash them (more on that later).
- Treasure cars are a great addition, though they also highlight how mild the world was when they’re found in convenience store parking lots, behind a golf club office, and under a bridge.
- The aftermarket cars parked out in the world for purchase are varied and surprising, always worth pulling over to take a look.
- The unmarked trails in *Horizon 6* are the best they’ve ever been, and I love exploring them to the point that I wish there was an end-game map for them.
- As underwhelming as Legend Island is – and it’s *really* underwhelming – it’s a concrete goal that’s nice to have, though the game doth protest too much that it’s “not the end but a new beginning”. I can’t remember if any other *Horizon* game had something similar; I simply stopped playing them when I’d had my fill.

The structure of *Horizon 6* has been carefully refined and maximized, and that’s great. The car selection is massive as always, the music selection is good, all fine there. So what’s the problem? This game feels trapped and underfed. The trappings and world of *Horizon 6* are like microwaved, unseasoned chicken breast.

One of the “story” mission sets is to help a photographer get on the cover of a famous Japanese motor magazine. The third mission in this playlist is to ramp a *Dukes of Hazard*-esque muscle car and take a photo of it midair. The game spawns you between two rural warehouses and you limply ramp [into just some field](https://youtu.be/C9JDp7hi7iw?si=4mXZboFYNXQTLJ73&t=836). This is what passes for a “chapter”; this is the high-adrenaline, saccharine love letter to car culture. It is one of the thinnest, most boring things I’ve ever played. 

It’s not an outlier. The “story” content, ill-advised to start, is all like this. Every line of spoken dialog – and there’s weirdly a lot of it – is agony. (Shoutout to Amy Simpson, host of Horizon Pulse, the only high energy character in the whole game.) Every objective is to drive from point A to point B unperturbed.

Admittedly the list of verbs in a *Forza* game are limited, but when placed in a monotone Tokyo and repetitive countryside, filled with the same convenience stores, construction yards (these really bother me – why do  I want to see this??), half-cut forests (again, why??), and highway bridges, I’m frustrated by the lack of imagination.  I can *see* Mt. Fuji, but I can’t go there. And by “see”, I mean look at a low-rez JPEG of it in the skybox. *Horizon 5* had a volcano that took almost five minutes to reach the top of and was a adrenaline high to drift or scramble your way down. One of the oldest bragging points in games is “if you can see it, you can go there”; not the case here.

I’m not at all equipped to judge if *Horizon 6* portrays Japan accurately *per se*, but it doesn’t do so passionately, that’s for sure. It barely even registers on the [“Thing, Japan”](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/thing-japan) scale after the cherry blossoms drift across your screen in the intro. Again, the bland politeness of it all is baffling – I’m sure Japan has no history of tricking out their cars and doing anything illegal and super cool with them.

If I had to commit the sin of armchair designer – listen, I have a degree in this, inexplicably – I’d guess that Playground Games got put on the absurdly ambitious upcoming *Fable* and poor Turn 10 Studios was left to deliver a *Horizon* game mostly by itself. That sounds brutal, and would explain the restraint and gaps in technical polish – the whole game shimmers with upscaling, with traffic popping in a few feet in front of my bumper.

But there’s a glimmer of hope, and a pitch to be made.

The [wristband events](https://techwiser.com/forza-horizon-6-wristband-events/) in *Horizon 6* are a perfect dichotomy of the game’s highs and lows. “Launch Control” is a highly technical, bombastic drift challenge against a rocket launch countdown and it **rules**. It put a huge grin on face and sweat on my controller, *Forza* at its best.

But then, “Mech My Day” is a cringeworthy and spiceless “race” against a Kirkland’s Best Gundam. Bad dialog, uninteresting route,  generally lame interpretation of culture, and an unacceptable level of silliness. 

Hours later, while hunting down the regional mascots, I noticed that their cute lil’ faces change when smashed. It’s a split-second moment, one you won’t even notice unless you go into photo mode or have your camera pointed backwards, but it made me furrow my brow.

The *Horizon* series has at times had a loose grip on reality, but stuff like the LEGO and Hot Wheels expansions have been deliberately sequestered to their own separate maps. That line was clumsily blurred in *Horizon 6*; maybe it’s time to shake off the shackles. 

Realistically, where does *Horizon* go next? I’d love to see an entry set somewhere in Africa – rally races in the savannah, cross country next to a herd of wildebeest, ramp off Mount Kilimanjaro, drift through Marrakech, etc. – but I imagine cultural sensitivities might preclude a predominantly Scottish developer from tackling it. I can hear the executive meetings expressing concerns about “our target audience”. It’s not like they’ve nailed it in recent entries anyways – *Horizon 5* was littered with poorly-received Spanglish, as a small example.

So instead, why not leave the real world behind and go nuts? One version is that the Horizon festival was bankrolled by only cool billionaires and they built a whole island just for you and your friends, with every biome imaginable in impossible density. Another version is to tilt towards *Trackmania* – the best races and playground spaces in *Horizon 6* feature steel plates as roads that feel similarly improvised – and go big. Megastructures, underwater tunnels, colosseums, anti-gravity, goos, goblins, shrink rays, the kitchen sink. Call it a “simulation” and let the cars ground it – I want to drift a WRX STi through a level that looks stolen from *Hydro Thunder*.

The wristband event that takes you to Legend Island – which I really have to emphasize, is *so lame* – feels like an attempt at an “Avengers, assemble!” moment but with NPCs I don’t remember. Not only was it boring in the ways explained above, but it felt weirdly funereal, like the folks involved don’t think they’re going to get to do another *Horizon* game and this was the last hurrah. Maybe that’s true – between car and music licensing, it feels like these games are becoming [increasingly ephemeral](https://support.forza.net/hc/en-us/articles/30662484986387-Forza-Horizon-4-Delisting-FAQ), which might hurt the P&L statement that Microsoft executives grind into a fine powder and snort, hoping to give them powers like spice from *Dune*. 

I hope it isn’t, but if the choice is none or to be as compromised and milquetoast as *Horizon 6* revealed itself to be, maybe that’s for the better. Then again, imagine what they could do if they went wild – what could they put on the horizon, if it could be anything?
