A Windows 7 Shrine

I really freaking miss Windows 7. I installed Release Candidate 1 (RC1, released April 30, 2009) on our family computer without asking, knowing damn well that if I accidentally erased the main XP partition again I'd be in so much trouble. I burned that shit to a DVD and got going.

The smooth glass, the round Windows logo in the start button, the transparent windows; everything that was oblong and beveled and beige in XP was now reflective, beautifully curved, and contemporary. I adored it from the moment I got it to boot. (Thank goodness I got it to boot.)

Still, I was running Windows 7 RC1 on an already-aged HP Pavilion desktop with an XFX NVIDIA card I can't even remember the model number of. I do remember praising the gods that I had accidentally gotten my dad to buy the right one when we discovered, as we plugged it in, that it was AGP interface, not PCI. (I also remember it not being one of the cool cards that had a character on the box or heatsink , which was a bummer.)

I used to run a utility called AlacrityPC so I could play Crysis at maybe 25 frames per second (remember Fraps ?) at 1024x768 on our square flat-panel 17" Dell monitor. It shut down just about every part of the operating system that wasn't spitting out frames for games: stop all the services, don't render the wallpaper, kill explorer.exe. It temporarily deleted as much of Windows as it could so I could lose myself in Fallout 3 and Far Cry 2 .

Of recent relevance, I recklessly installed Windows 7 because I was excited for it. We had skipped over Vista by accident really, but the PC enthusiast world – magazines like Maximum PC and PC Gamer – was pumped for a return to form, for a fundamentally new look and feel for an operating system. Nowadays I do my best to avoid the OS as much as I can. (Update: I switched off Windows entirely in 2025, switching to macOS for daily driving and Linux for gaming.)

Windows 7 was excited for you to come on in and make yourself comfortable, and I miss that.
