A Windows 7 Shrine

I really freaking miss Windows 7. I installed Release Candidate 1 (RC1, released April 30, 2009) on our family computer without asking, knowing damn well that if I accidentally erased the main windows XP partition again I'd be in so much trouble. I burned that shit to a DVD and got cracking.

The smooth glass, the round Windows logo in the start button, the transparent windows; everything that was oblong and beveled and beige in XP was now reflective, razor-sharp, and and contemporary. I adored it from the moment I got it to boot. (Thank goodness I got it to boot.)

Still, I was running Windows 7 RC1 on an already-aged HP Pavilion desktop with an NVIDIA graphics card I can't even remember the model number of. I do remember praising the gods that I had accidentally gotten my dad to buy the right one when we discovered, as we plugged it in, that it was AGP interface, not PCI. (I also remember it not being one of the cool cards that had a character on the box or heatsink .)

I used to run a utility called AlacrityPC so I could play Crysis (2007) at maybe 25 frames per second at 1024x768 on our flat-panel, square, 17" Dell monitor. What did the utility do? Well, it shut down just about every part of the operating system that wasn't spitting out frames of games: stop all the services, cease rendering the wallpaper, shut down explorer.exe. It temporarily deleted as much of Windows 7 as it could so I could play Fallout 3 and Far Cry 2 .

Of particular recent relevance, I recklessly installed Windows 7 because I was excited for it. We had skipped over Vista for a host of reasons, but the PC enthusiast world – which I had access to via magazines like Maximum PC and PC Gamer , was pumped for a return to form, for a fundamentally look and feel for an operating system. Nowadays I do my best to avoid the OS as much as I can, and use utilities to scrape the goo off its surface to make it more palatable.

Windows 7 was excited for you to come on in and make yourself comfortable, and I miss that.
